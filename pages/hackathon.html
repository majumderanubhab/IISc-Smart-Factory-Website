Innovation for MSME 4.0: National Hackathon Series 2025

Empowering Indian Manufacturing through Digital Innovation

About the Hackathons

The Innovation for MSME 4.0: National Hackathon Series 2025 is a flagship initiative supported by the Ministry of Heavy Industries (MHI), Government of India, and organised by Indian Institute of Science.

The series aims to identify and nurture innovative technology-based solutions that will help Indian Micro, Small, and Medium Enterprises (MSMEs) accelerate digital transformation and the adoption of Industry 4.0 technologies.

  • Each Hackathon will be an independent 2-day event bringing together innovators, researchers, start-ups, and MSME collaborators to develop actionable prototypes and business concepts that enhance the competitiveness of Indian manufacturing.
  • The most promising teams will be incubated at Indian Institute of Science under the MHI-supported MSME 4.0 Innovation Programme.

Mentor Allocation

  • 30 problem statements → 30 incubate teams
  • Each problem statement assigned 1 mentor based on expertise.
  • Mentors guide teams through idea validation, prototyping, and pitching.
  • Mentors may be distributed across domains to ensure balanced support.

Hackathon General Rules

Hackathon Rules & Regulations

# Criteria Name Description
1 Eligibility The hackathon is open to students, professionals, researchers, and startups. Participants may register as teams of 2–5 members. All team members must be legally 18 years or older at the time of registration.
2 Registration & Team Formation Registration must be completed before the official deadline on the hackathon website. Late entries will not be entertained. Each participant can be part of only one team. Multiple registrations by the same individual may result in disqualification. Team composition (members, roles, chosen problem statement) is frozen after confirmation. Any change requires prior approval from organizers.
3 Problem Statement Selection Each team must choose one problem statement from the published list of 50. Problem allocation may be adjusted by organizers to ensure balanced distribution. Teams cannot change their selected problem statement once the hackathon begins.
4 Hackathon Structure The hackathon will be conducted in three stages: Round 1 – Idea Pitch; Round 2 – Prototype Development; Round 3 – Final Pitch. All submissions must be made before the deadline announced during the event. Late submissions will not be considered.
5 Code of Conduct Participants must adhere to principles of fair play, integrity, and professionalism. Disrespectful behavior, harassment, discrimination, or inappropriate language will result in immediate disqualification. Participants must comply with all venue rules, including safety, security, and IT usage policies. Alcohol, drugs, or prohibited substances are strictly banned during the event.
6 Use of Resources Participants may use open-source libraries, frameworks, and publicly available APIs, provided they comply with licensing terms. Use of pre-existing proprietary software, hardware, or code is not permitted unless explicitly declared and approved. Teams must develop the majority of their solution during the hackathon timeframe. Pre-developed projects presented as hackathon output will be disqualified.
7 Mentorship & External Assistance Each team will be assigned a mentor for domain-specific guidance. Mentors may suggest directions and validate feasibility but are not allowed to develop or deliver code/prototypes for teams. External consultation beyond mentors is not allowed unless approved by the organizers.
8 Deliverables Each team must submit the following:
  • Idea Pitch Deck (Round 1) – problem statement, solution approach, feasibility.
  • Prototype / MVP (Round 2) – working demo or functional PoC.
  • Final Presentation (Round 3) – prototype demo, business model, scalability plan.
  • Source Code & Documentation – to be shared with organizers for validation.
All deliverables must be in English and submitted through the official hackathon portal.
9 Judging & Evaluation Judging will be based on the following weighted criteria: Innovation & Creativity (20%), Technical Feasibility (20%), Business Viability (20%), Scalability & Impact (20%), Presentation & Teamwork (20%). Judges’ decisions are final and binding. No appeals will be entertained. Tiebreakers may be decided by an additional Q&A round with shortlisted teams.

If selected for incubation, IP terms (ownership, licensing, or revenue-sharing, if any) will be defined through a separate legal agreement with the incubator. By participating, teams grant organizers the right to use names, photos, solution descriptions, and demo videos for promotional purposes.
11 Confidentiality & Data Usage Any datasets provided by organizers must be used only for hackathon purposes. Teams must not disclose confidential data or proprietary information of the organizers, mentors, or partners. Violation may result in legal action.

FAQs

  • What is the overall format of the hackathon?
    The hackathon runs for 2–3 days, featuring approximately 50 curated problem statements across domains such as IoT, AI, Cybersecurity, and Manufacturing. It consists of three rounds: Idea Pitch, Prototype/PoC development, and Final Pitch presenting the solution and business impact.
  • Who is eligible to participate?
    Open to students, professionals, researchers, and startups. All team members must be 18 years or older at registration.
  • How do participants register and form teams?
    Registration is via the official website and accepts individuals or teams of 2–5 members. Teams choose one problem statement from the published list; organizers may adjust allocations to balance team distributions.
  • What if team sizes vary significantly?
    Ideal team sizes are 2–5 or 4–7 members. Larger teams may limit active contributors per task; smaller or solo participants may merge into larger teams or receive extra support.
  • What are the technical eligibility requirements?
    Participants must have skills relevant to their chosen problem statement. Skillsets and interests are collected during registration, and organizers may guide or flag teams with insufficient expertise.
  • What support is provided before and during the event?
    Pre-hackathon webinars and Q&A sessions explain problem statements, judging criteria, incubation pathways, and mentor guidelines. Assigned mentors provide ongoing domain guidance without coding.
  • How are mentors allocated and managed?
    One mentor is assigned per problem or cluster of related problems, providing rotational and virtual guidance to cover all teams effectively.
  • What deliverables are expected from each team?
    Teams must submit:
    • Round 1: Idea Pitch Deck (problem statement, solution approach, feasibility)
    • Round 2: Working Prototype/MVP demo
    • Round 3: Final Presentation including prototype demo, business model, scalability plan
    • Source Code and documentation
  • What are the judging criteria?
    Judging is based on five equally weighted categories: Innovation & Creativity, Technical Feasibility, Business Viability, Scalability & Sustainability, and Presentation & Teamwork, each contributing 20%.
  • What happens if a team cannot complete a prototype?
    Teams may submit detailed designs, simulations, or proof of concept documents. Judges consider technical feasibility and team effort as part of evaluation.
  • Are there rules regarding the use of software, code, and resources?
    Open-source libraries, publicly available APIs, and provided datasets may be used respecting licensing. Proprietary or pre-built projects require explicit approval. Majority of development must happen during hackathon timeframe.
  • Can teams consult outside mentors or experts?
    Only assigned mentors may provide guidance. External consultation is disallowed unless approved by organizers.
  • How is intellectual property (IP) handled?
    Teams retain IP rights over their creations. IP terms for incubation are governed by separate agreements. Organizers can use names, photos, and demo content for promotion with team consent.
  • What if external services fail during the event?
    Organizers are not responsible for failures of internet, electricity, or other external services but will try to assist where possible.
  • How are disputes or misconduct handled?
    A strict code of conduct applies. Violations (harassment, discrimination, unprofessional behavior) may lead to disqualification. Organizers mediate disputes; decisions are final.
  • How is confidentiality maintained?
    Teams must use datasets only for hackathon purposes and keep organizer or partner data confidential. Legal actions apply on violations.
  • How are winners selected and recognized?
    Top 3–5 teams and special category awards are announced with certificates, cash prizes, and incubation offers.
  • What does post-hackathon incubation involve?
    Promising teams receive mentorship and provide support for seed funding, support in industry connections and pilot runs, and 3–6 months of monitoring from the start of the program.
  • How is management of ~50 problem statements handled?
    Organizers balance team distribution across problem statements, merging or reallocating teams to lesser-chosen problems to ensure competition.
  • What if some problems have very few participants?
    Problem statements may be combined or teams encouraged to change their selection early in registration to maintain problem engagement.
  • How are incomplete teams or dropouts mitigated?
    Pre-event orientation stresses commitment. Standby participants and team merges help address dropouts. Mentors conduct checkpoints to identify issues early.
  • Can teams change members or problem statements mid-event?
    Team compositions are frozen after confirmation; changes require PIO/organizer approval and are discouraged.
  • What if a team member leaves during the event?
    Replacement is possible with organizer consent.
  • Are presentations public?
    Final presentations are delivered before a jury panel and may be open to mentors and other participants.
  • Is networking facilitated?
    Multiple sessions for informal networking with mentors, organizers, industry guests, and incubators are planned.
  • Are there participation fees?

    One mentor per problem or related problem cluster provides rotational support to manage mentoring workload effectively.
  • What fallback resources exist if hardware or APIs fail?
    Cloud tools, open-source alternatives, and simulations are recommended fallback options.
  • Can teams submit alternative approaches if prototyping fully isn't feasible?
    Yes, simulations, conceptual designs, and documentation are acceptable alternatives.
  • How are remote participation and time zones managed for hackathon?
    Remote participation is not encouraged.
  • What coding and presentation languages are allowed?
    Any coding language is allowed; presentations and documentation must be in English.
  • How are IP disputes among team members resolved?
    Teams should agree on IP ownership beforehand; organizers provide guidance but do not mediate legal disputes.
  • Can teams submit fixes after the deadline?
    No late submissions are allowed; incubation phase may allow post-event improvements.
  • Is participant feedback provided after the event?
    Feedback sessions or reports from judges and mentors are offered post-event.
  • What ensures judging fairness and transparency?
    Final decision will be by Jury at IISc, according to the established, balanced criteria
  • How are technical submission issues handled?
    Teams must ensure proper formatting and functionality pre-deadline; support is offered before but not after the deadline.
